Bridge Vulnerability

Bridge vulnerability represents the bridge’s physical condition exposed to future acute shock. The physical condition is the relative measure of a bridge’s structural health and condition, and it represents the level of service (LOS) of a bridge toward the acute shocks. Bridge vulnerability can be evaluated by real time data generation approaches using field instruments and remote sensing techniques (Nassif et al. 2003, 2017). To assess the bridge’s performance, finite element models can be developed and calibrated using collected real time data for generating further simulation data. However, this methodology requires exorbitant computational efforts and technical expertise. However, wireless sensing techniques using various field instruments such as accelerometers, strain gauges, thermocouples, dynamic strain transducers, displacement sensors, vibrometers, and tiltmeters provide inputs to analyze the current health condition of structures (Bhowmik et al. 2019). By using these inputs, various mathematical theories (Kalman filter method, Eigen perturbation method, and Eigen decomposition method.) can be applied to detect real time damage detection in civil structures (Hsieh et al. 2006; Roy et al. 2012; Noel et al. 2017; Bhowmik et al. 2019, 2020). These techniques require low computational efforts, and they are efficient and user-friendly. However, due to budget and time constraints, this study assessed bridge vulnerability based on the subjective judgments of bridge owners and experts.

Prediction of acute shock severity

Prediction of acute shock severity represents the probable intensity of future acute shocks, as predicted by the bridge owners. The occurrence of acute shock intensity affects the LOS of bridges. Dong and Frangopol (2015) state that the probability of incremental damage to the vulnerable structure depends on these verities of acute shock. Basic judgment and review of historical cases are required to predict the severity of future acute shock in a particular region. Various organizations attempted to define severity and its level based on the nature and intensity of acute shocks.

Average Daily Traffic

Average Daily Traffic (ADT) is the assessment of the traffic flow of a particular bridge. ADT describes the social losses (Andric and Lu 2017) and shows the importance of a bridge in the transportation network (Minaie and Moon 2017). Moreover, ADT signifies the LOS of a bridge based on the flow of traffic (Deco et al. 2013) and, therefore, represents the societal value of a bridge to the community.

Preliminary Restoration Cost

Preliminary restoration cost includes the initial estimate of repair/reconstruction, removal of debris, or construction of a temporary bypass after an acute shock (Deco et al. 2013; Andric and Lu 2017). The preliminary restoration cost is associated with monetary values which are, in general, based on the deterioration of the bridge’s structural condition. Therefore, the preliminary restoration cost is one of the important factors in robustness assessment because it signifies the importance of a bridge by indicating that the repair losses associated with repairs in the aftermath of certain catastrophes are proportional to the costs of reconstructing the bridge.

Restoration Time

The restoration time represents the possible time required for restoration work to return a bridge to full and normal working conditions. In general, the restoration time is recognized in multiple stages (Karamlou and Bocchini 2015; Karamlou and Bocchini 2017; Andric and Lu 2017). This study prefers singlepath restoration due to its simplicity and practicality when computing the BRI (Minaie and Moon 2017). The estimation of specific restoration time depends on the experts’ judgments, organization policies, emergency repair contracts, and historical cases. Therefore, the restoration time is arguably a technical factor in the evaluation of bridge resilience.

Disaster Management

Disaster management practice refers to the acute shock preparedness programs, educational programs, scheduled tests, and drill programs conducted by the bridge owners in the preparedness for acute shocks (Minaie and Moon 2017; Andric and Lu 2017). This factor represents preparedness data aggregated by the bridge’s administrator (public or private). The 10 criteria considered in this study are based on DMP (2018) and Minaie and Moon (2017), and they are outlined as follows: (1) community extreme event education programs, scheduled test and drill programs; (2) designated evacuation routes; (3) designated shelters at a high point (more than high flood level) and near to the bridge; (4) disaster management plans and designated centers; (5) first responders equipped with the necessary tools and equipment such as inflatable boats, rescues rope, life jackets, life buoys, reflective tapes, and submergible search lights to manage the postextreme event conditions; (6) a list of contact number of emergency contractors for incident management; (7) local access to equipment (such as debris removal, crane, and excavator), goods, and materials for minimal restorations; (8) available modes of transportation for individuals >2 (5 km radius around the bridge); (9) access to a number of emergency facilities (including emergency, hospital, fuel stations): ≥5 nos. (5 km radius around the bridge); and (10) sirens blown, tape recording announcements, and newspaper advertisement to warn communities of floods, cyclones, and electrical power-cuts. All 10 criteria have their purposes because they are linked with the administrators of other infrastructures. Therefore, if the majority of the criteria (listed previously) are satisfied by bridge administrator, then it will aid them with the preparedness of bridge maintenance against acute shock and ensure that the bridges in question recover functionality faster.

Area Or Region Affected

The area or region affected represents the area or region influenced by an acute shock. Different acute shocks with different intensities affected different areas or regions (Minaie and Moon 2017). For example, earthquakes and tsunamis typically affect large areas, whereas floods and landslides affect relatively small areas. Duration For Procuring And Tendering

The duration for procuring and tendering refers to the time taken for preparing procurement and offering tenders for repair and maintenance work. This factor is dependent on organization policies and procedures. Therefore, if more time is taken to prepare procurement and offer tenders, the speed of recovery of bridge services will be affected adversely.

Inspection Technique

The inspection technique refers to the application of different inspection techniques to assess the physical condition of bridges. These techniques are either an objective or a subjective approach (Minaie and Moon 2017). The objective approach includes nondestructive testing (NDT), underwater testing, and analytical procedures for condition assessment and the subjective approach includes the visual inspection procedures for condition assessment of bridges. As mentioned in Table 3, when a bridge owner uses more techniques such as visual inspection, analytical techniques, NDT, and underwater inspection for the physical condition assessment of any bridge it increases the level of preciseness and reliability (level 5) in the physical condition assessment of bridges and vice versa.

Maturity In Planning And Scheduling

Maturity in planning and scheduling refers to the use of building information modeling (BIM) by the bridge owner for the planning and scheduling of repair/reconstruction work. McGuire et al. (2016) developed a method for tracking and assessing the health condition of bridges using BIM. This method expedites the inspection and evaluation processes of a bridge’s condition during its life cycle. In the future, it might result in more automated practice even though the use of BIM is not a regular practice in bridge engineering and management currently. However, Barlish and Sullivan (2012) have already presented the BIM maturity level and described the benefit of BIM utilization in any construction project. Table 4 gives four BIM maturity levels. Consideration of this organizational factor might encourage bridge owners to maximize applications of BIM in their bridge projects.

Emergency Response Management

The emergency response management (ERM) level refers to the resources used for the effective, efficient, and standardized operation of a transportation network (Freckleton et al. 2012). It provides the real time mobilization of traffic and demand of the bridge during and after an acute shock. Freckleton et al. (2012) and Minaie and Moon (2017) presented five levels of ERM as outlined in Table 5. Table 5 explains that the intelligent transportation and advanced traveler information systems (level 5) ensure more effective, efficient, and standardized operation of transportation networks during and after acute shock. The level 1 technique might have more difficulty in managing the traffic than the level 5 technique. Unlike traffic police (level 1), modern traveler information systems (levels 4 and 5) direct drivers in advance to less congested alternative routes, therefore, relieving congestion in that part of the traffic network. Hence, ERM is considered as a part of resourcefulness because its activities, methods, procedures, and tools can help to manage the traffic during and after the disaster

Arrangement Funds

Arrangements of funds refers to the adjustment of the financial budget for repair/reconstruction work. The adjustment is required only if there is a strain in the available budget. Therefore, the factor is important for resilience planning because it will reduce the strain on the budget available and maximize the option for the bridge owner to respond to the consequences of acute shocks.

Availability Of Materials And Equipments

Accessible material and equipment refer to the availability of materials and equipment within the local transportation network. Their availability should be easy and quick from the local area. For example, materials such as steel or precast girders, piles, and bearings should be easily accessible within the local area surrounding a particular bridge.

Availability Of Backup Contractor

Availability of a backup contractor refers to the availability of disaster response contractors within the local area. The bridge owner should have a list of the registered, experienced, and competent contractors who can execute repair and rehabilitation work after an acute shock or loss of functionality. This factor increases redundancy by ensuring that there are several backup contractors during emergencies.

Detour Length

Detour length refers to the diversion route around an affected bridge to the ADT or community of a specified area (Minaie and Moon 2017). It is essential to consider this factor in the determination of bridge resilience because it represents indirect losses incurred when a bridge is dysfunctional or fails (Andric and Lu 2017).

Availability Of Fund

Availability of funds refers to the financial budget that is available to the bridge owner for the repair and reconstruction work of a bridge. Deficiencies in the available budget affect the redundancy measure because they limit the options for repair and reconstruction works. Hence, if there is a strain on the availability of the budget, it will affect the recovery of a bridge.
