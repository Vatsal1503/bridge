Introduction

Bridges are crucial infrastructures in any road and rail network. They minimize travel costs by providing the amenities across inaccessible terrain, areas of high altitude, and above waterbodies. Therefore, the dysfunction or failure of a bridge significantly impacts commerce and services and most important risks the loss of human life (Venkittaraman and Banerjee 2014). These negative consequences occur mainly after an acute shock such as earthquakes, floods, cyclones, and tsunamis. Although bridges rarely fail when they do the results are catastrophic. Therefore, vulnerable, dysfunctional, and failed bridges decrease the efficiency of a transportation network.

Previous research (Singh et al. 2002; Sheth et al. 2006; GFDRR 2010; Kumar et al. 2013; Kala 2014; UNDP 2018) cumulatively reported 3,709 bridges that were damaged due to different acute shocks such as earthquakes, tsunami, and floods occurring from 2001 to 2018 in various parts of India. Similarly, Wardhana and Hadipriono (2003) analyzed bridge failure incidents in the US between 1989 and 2000, concluding that almost 53% of bridge failures resulted from floods, and the effects of these such as scour, debris, and drift. All the previously mentioned examples have one thing in common, the bridges are all deficient in resilience.

Resilience, if properly understood, measured, and fortified can mitigate the negative effects of acute shocks. In recent years, India has focused on constructing resilient infrastructure and cities. Four Indian cities Chennai, Jaipur, Pune, and Surat, are members of the 100-Resilient Cities (2013) (100-RC), which are pioneered by the Rockefeller Foundation. In addition, the government of India has started a nongovernmental organization (NGO) named Resilient India (2018). Resilient India works in the field of disaster risk reduction and crime prevention. In addition, Gupta (2007), Shaw et al. (2010), and Sharma and Singh (2015) worked in the area of urban and community resilience, which is suffering in the face of uncertainty and climate change. However, the resilience assessment of bridges across the globe (including India) has only been explored a little (Banerjee et al. 2019).

Considering the importance of resilience in infrastructure management, this study aims to develop a comprehensive analytical approach for assessing the resilience of bridges accounting for the impact of floods only. To gain a better understanding of bridge resilience and approach its assessment, the following sections review the literature and outline gaps in the existing research. Then, the sections include the objectives of this study, research methodology, validation of this study, and the conclusions.
